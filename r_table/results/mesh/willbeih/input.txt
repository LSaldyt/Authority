This summary of the family's characters also reviews as completely as possible the literature already published. In chapter IV there is a discussion of the characters in regard of their significance for taxonomy (IV/1), phylogeny (IV/3) and systematic position (IV/4) of the family. Informations about geographical distribution have been considered and partly completed, and the distribution has for the first time been mapped for all species. Inferring from the final state we find the tuber consisting aboveall of a disc-shaped thickened internod, which follows the prophyll and is partly adnate to it. There is a continuous series of reduction from a panicle which is composed of many spices (e. g. Orthothylax) to a solitary spice with only a few flowers (Philydrella). The terminology suggested by Troll is adopted for an exact description of inflorescences (Abb. 3). Contrary to the hitherto published interpretation of the floral diagram of Philydraceae this paper proves that the large adaxial perianth segment consists of 3 (not 2) organs: 2 leaves of the outer perianth whorl and the median member of the inner whorl, which accordingly is not abortive. Above all morphology (partly 3 apices!), ontogeny and innervation serve as a proof. There is no clue to the interpretation of the inner perianth segments as staminodia. Transitions to stamina, as mentioned in the literature with regard to Philydrum, has never been observed; but this species sometimes possesses -- in addition to the normal stamen -- a second with a small or undeveloped anther. There is a detailed description of the concrescences between the perianth members, the filament and the ovary, besides the symmetry of the ovary and the placentation. With regard to several characters of the fruit and seed the genera differ in a characteristic manner. The embryology of Philydrum lanuginosum has been examined from the first differentiation of the anther and the ovules to the structure of the ripe seed. Only 3 items may be stressed here again: (1) The pollen grains of all 5 species are monocolpate; this applies also -- contrary to earlier statements -- to those of Helmholtzia acorifolia. Only the pollen grains of Philydrum remain coherent in tetrads. (2) The development of endosperm accords with the helobial type. It is characterised by the fact that cell walls are formed much earlier in the small, chalazal chamber than in the large, micropylar chamber. Swamy's and Parameswaran's supposition (1963), that in the monocotyledons (contrary to some dicotyledons) the chalazal chamber -- if at all -- becomes cellular later than the micropylar chamber, does not turn to be true with regard to the Philydraceae. The next longitudinal division of the two daughter cells of ca, resulting in the formation of the quadrant q, is asymmetric. Without any previous formation of an octant (in so far comparable to the Juncus-variation of the Onagrad-type) begins now the formation of the primary dermatogen; this is completed however, only in 2 of the 4 quadrant-cells in one periclinally division. Two divisions are necessary in the 2 other quadrant cells, which are firstly divided by an oblique -- anticlinally directed wall (see Abb. 30). Also the further divisions in the two quadrant-sectors, marked with dots in Abb. 30, differ quite regularly from the two unmarked-ones. In a transverse section the cells are arranged in a rotational-symmetric structure, which is superimposed by the dorsiventral symmetry of the older embryo. In pursuance of this very regular sequence of divisions the share of the quadrant sectors in the parts of the final embryo can exactly be fixed. The fewcelled shoot apex develops from the basal cells of but one sector, the ontogenetically genuinely terminal cotyledon from cells of all four sectors, the procambium is formed by central cells of only 2 sectors. -- The basal cell (cb) produces only part of the primary root and the mostly 2-celled, weakly developed suspensor. There is no evidence to the existence of a single apical cell in the root apex of the embryo. In Philydrum and Philydrella autogamy was also proved cytologically; in Orthothylax and Helmholtzia it must be assumed as very probable. The haploid chromosome number of Philydrum lanuginosum is n = 8, of Orthothylax glaberrimus n = 16. The Philydraceae are a natural, homogenous family, characterised by a combination of highly specialized and relatively primitive features. Within the family the very nearly related genera Helmholtzia and Orthothylax are more primitive than the equally nearly related genera Philydrum and Philydrella, though the level of specialisation is not the same in all characters. With regard to embryology the Philydraceae correspond more with the Pontederiaceae and Bromeliaceae (partly also Juncaceae) than with the Commelinaceae, Orchidaceae and other families which are compared here. In consideration of many characters -- as more closely established in Chapter IV/4 -- there seem to exist relations the easiest to the Pontederiaceae, perhaps also to the Haemodoraceae and Hypoxidaceae of Hutchinson's Heamodorales.
